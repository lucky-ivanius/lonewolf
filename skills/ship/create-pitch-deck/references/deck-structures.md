# Deck structures by audience

Three 10-slide structures. Use the structure that matches the audience and resist mixing.

## Judges (hackathon)

Order optimized for a 3 to 5 minute live pitch with judges scanning many submissions.

1. Hook. The user, the painful current behavior, the new outcome. One sentence.
2. Problem. Concrete, named, with a small piece of evidence (a quote from a candidate user, a measured cost).
3. Solution. What the product does, end to end, in one sentence per step.
4. Demo. A screenshot of the load-bearing flow at its success state. The narrator describes the sequence.
5. Why now. The single capability (a new model, a new protocol, a new distribution channel) that makes this implementation possible today and not two years ago.
6. Traction or signal. Even a small signal: 5 candidate users in `will-pay.md`, 1 testnet integration partner, 100 testnet transactions. Refuse to fake.
7. Team. Names, roles, why this team. One slide.
8. Business model. Pulled from `business-model.md`. If the verdict is `no`, the slide is honest about that.
9. Ask. What the user wants from the audience: votes, a partnership intro, a follow-up call.
10. Close. The hook, restated, with the demo URL and contact.

## Investors (seed, pre-seed)

Order optimized for a 10 to 15 minute meeting where the investor needs to underwrite the team and the bet.

1. Hook. One sentence.
2. Market. The size and shape of the market the project addresses. Cited.
3. Problem. The painful behavior, with the cost named.
4. Solution. The product in one paragraph plus a screenshot.
5. Traction. Real numbers, cited. If pre-launch, name the demand signal.
6. Business model. Cited from `business-model.md` and `will-pay.md`. Cite the experiment.
7. Why now. A change in market, tech, or behavior that makes this possible only now.
8. Team. Names, roles, prior work. Why this team and not someone else.
9. Ask. Round size, instrument, target close date.
10. Close. The hook, restated, with the contact.

## Grant (ecosystem foundations, protocol grant programs)

Order optimized for written review by a grant committee. The deck is read, not pitched.

1. Hook. One sentence.
2. Problem. The named problem in the target ecosystem this project addresses.
3. Solution. The product in one paragraph plus a screenshot.
4. Why this team. What this team has shipped and why they will deliver.
5. Deliverables. Three to five concrete deliverables with definitions of done.
6. Milestones. Dates, gates, payouts. Each milestone has a measurable outcome.
7. Budget. Line items, dollar amounts, cited.
8. Public-good rationale. What the ecosystem gets that it could not get from a closed equivalent. Open source license, public artifacts, measurable benefit.
9. Sustainability. How the project continues after the grant period. Honest answer, not "we will figure it out".
10. Ask. Grant size, milestone schedule, point of contact.

## Slide-level rules across all three audiences

- One claim per slide. The claim survives if you remove the slide.
- On-slide copy is short; the narrator carries the rest.
- Visuals are real screenshots from the project's load-bearing flow, not stock images.
- No banned words, no em-dashes, product and protocol names capitalized consistently.
