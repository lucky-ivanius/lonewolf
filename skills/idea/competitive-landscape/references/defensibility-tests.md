# Defensibility tests

Apply these to the candidate's angle to test whether the differentiation holds.

## The one-sentence test

Can the user state the angle in one sentence that names a specific competitor and a specific advantage? If not, sharpen until they can.

Bad: "We have better UX than QuickBooks."
Better: "We generate the invoice from the client's Slack thread automatically; QuickBooks makes the freelancer re-type everything into a form."

The bad version is generic. The better version is specific (named mechanism, named competitor, named advantage).

## The cheap-extension test

Can an existing incumbent add the candidate's feature in a sprint?

If the answer is yes, the moat is not the feature; it is timing, distribution, or audience. The user must articulate which.

If the answer is no (because the feature requires data the incumbent does not have, a business model that would cannibalize them, or a different mental model), the moat may be structural. Confirm by naming the mechanism.

## The clone test

Can a competent dev with a coding agent clone the visible product in a weekend?

Assume yes for almost every app in the AI era. The follow-up question is the real test: what does the clone NOT get? (The audience, the data accumulated, the integrations approved, the trust earned, the distribution channel.) If the answer is "nothing", it's a feature, not a product worth launching on its own.

## The audience test

Can the user reach the audience? Or does the moat assume distribution that does not exist?

A clever feature without distribution loses to a generic feature with distribution every time.

## The status-quo test

Why would a user switch from whatever they do today — including doing nothing?

If the answer is a category label ("it's decentralized", "it's AI-powered"), the candidate has a problem. Users do not buy categories; they buy outcomes (speed, cost, ownership, less babysitting). Restate in outcome terms.

## The 6-month test

In 6 months, will the candidate's angle still be the angle? Or will the platform catch up, the incumbent extend, or the audience move?

A 6-month-stable angle is the bar. Anything shorter is a wedge that needs a follow-on plan.

## The 5-friend test

If the user described the angle to 5 people in the target market, would they nod or push back?

Push-back is often more useful than nodding. If 5 people all nod, the angle is too generic to test. If 1-2 push back hard, the user has identified the controversial premise; sharpen the angle around defending it.

## Anti-pattern angles

Reject these as defensible angles:

- "We are first to market." (Lasts weeks at best.)
- "We have a community." (Communities follow products, not vice versa, until late stage.)
- "We have better UX." (True for everyone; not differentiating.)
- "We are open source." (Most projects are.)
- "We will iterate faster." (Every founder believes this.)
- "We have a great team." (Necessary, not sufficient.)
- "We are AI-powered" or "AI-augmented" or any AI-prefixed claim with no substance.

Defensible angles name a specific user, a specific mechanism, a specific competitor, and a specific advantage. Less than that is positioning theater.
